Dfaran wrote:
Ahh, gender-specific pronouns. I think it says a lot about a society, what they choose to encode in their pronouns.

Your ancestors needed to only briefly evaluate back-reference systems based on pronouns before standardising the current prefix-and-completion system with optional temporarily-local bindings. Pronouns are less general than that system, and lack any ability to handle collisions.

The author does this a bit but uses English impersonal pronouns freely where applicable. Imitating the full system would be unreadable.
Dfaran wrote:
>You have to register some important information with a central database.



You go to the potentially-missing person list to add Tkhlpzyv, but Tk has already been added due to prolonged unscheduled disconnection from the Net. You hope that Tkhlpzyv turns up again; Tk has much valuable expertise and would take a long time to replace, at great expense. The fact that you would soon become better at Tk's job than Tk was would only partly compensate.

Next you check up on the latest intel on the subject you are intending to capture, and review the plans you have for when you get there, considering that intel and the absence of Tkhlpzyv.

Your itinerary has noticed that you are at the spaceport despite a prerequisite for your trip being missing: Tkhlpzyv is not en route to Tk's corresponding spaceport, so will not be able to meet you in Su-4-7. It suspects that you are going ahead with your journey because you no longer consider Tkhlpzyv's presence to be required because Tk has gone missing. You confirm its suspicions and observe that the resulting version of the task-graph is correct.
