orngjce223 wrote:
Jz: Wax philosophical about how the subject's definition of "normal" may differ from your definition of "normal". Indulge in wondering which one is more correct for a few moments, then realize what you're doing and make a note to readjust your mental norms again once you return to society.

LN-4-7-22 did not receive any brain-nurturing at the necessary stages, so some senses and actuators are missing: REMS, REMI, telekinesis. Without REMS it cannot directly observe the brains of others, making any interaction with citizens difficult (though humans leak some analogous information through indirect means such as body language). Without that and REMI, it cannot perform the detailed manipulation/examination of others' brains that is the duty of every citizen. Without telekinesis it can only move things by touching them, so it could not handle without injury anything that is too hot, or cold, or electrically charged, and cannot (e.g.) push buttons that are covered up to prevent accidental pressing. Without exposure to language, it has not encountered enough mental challenge to develop thinking skills. The last two would prevent it from using most technology designed for the fully-abled, even if it understood how.

In short, LN-4-7-22's idea of normal is a mentally-disabled state; it cannot become part of civilisation, and since it cannot perceive the moral forces which guide all citizens, it should not.

The grammatical rule is that "who" is used as a subject and "whom" is used as an object, whether of a verb or of a preposition (for, to, with, etc.). "Whoever" and "whomever" use the same rules - they're relatively recent contractions of who-ever and whom-ever.

So "to whomever" is correct at least in this case.
