    Maybe it is already wide enough, since you have passed this way once already. You experiment with the properties of the ceiling and floor.



    When pressed gently, the ceiling is tangible and feels like it is made of the same stone as the floor is, but under greater pressure it becomes intangible. The parts that have been passed through rapidly reset to the tangible state again. This low part of the ceiling therefore acts to disguise the fact that this passageway is traversable but does not impede such traversal much if it is attempted. By being a ceiling rather than a floor or wall, one cannot discover its properties accidentally using one's weight.

    Originally Posted by orngjce223
    > Is there a known material with that property?
    Not that you know of, in your brain or in the database fragments that you have. You would have automatically tried to connect to a remote databse to look it up, but you have now configured yourself not to do so. The material is almost certainly made by someone rather than being natural, and the humans have not shown the ability to make anything like it.

    Originally Posted by Isoraqathedh
    See if you can break a piece of soft-rock-hard-none[41] (surrounding rock optional) to play with. Or make Shewson play with it.

    ----

    [41] I'm gonna call that thing SRHN. Serhen. x Ha, ha, ha.

    x = Once upon a time there were three shrimps. Their names are
    Intangible shafts, structure, and cables would be very handy in building some 3D machinery.

    Cutting some off won't be easy though. The first thing you consider is simply isolating a tangible part by surrounding it with other material, but a thought experiment makes that seem unlikely: intangible parts remember their form and position relative to the tangible material, so when a piece of tangible material is partitioned then if the parts could be moved apart it is not clear which tangible section the intangible material would remain attached to.



    You try it to be certain; it doesn't work.

    You consider other possibilities, many of them impractical with your current (lack of) equipment:
    • Chemical or other nano-scale attack.
    • (If there is a delay in it becoming intangible) applying a very high pressure for a very short period of time.
    • Something that isn't a way to cut normal matter, e.g. removing a power supply.
    • Some way of preventing the intangibility, though the material might be just too strong for you to break anyway.


    • High-concentration telekinesis, if you can get the tools for it (your own telekinesis wouldn't be strong enough even if you still had it). Too much telekinetic force could just induce intangibility though.
    • Laser or other heat-based cutting.
    • Ionising radiation or ultra-fine REMI.
    • Intangibility is apparently induced by pressure rather than absolute force, so: exerting a large stress on the connection between a part of the material and the rest, but not high pressure at any point. That would need the insetion of an object which's surface area is high compared to the minimal area that can enclose it.
    And as a general consideration, if the intangible parts are weakened in tier bulk or their boundaries, that could be exploited.
