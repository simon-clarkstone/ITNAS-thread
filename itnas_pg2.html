The author sees that he has received one suggestion that is just fine and one which is most unsuitable for this story. Therefore, he will exercise his right to pedantry and point out that Tkhlpzyv has to make "a detour", singular.

Crowstone wrote:
this detour is to go to the nearest recreational center

You wonder if that is supposed to be an insult. The distinction between recreation and work is for those without true motivation. Knowing that what you are doing is productive (or good for you, or whatever) will cause you to like it. After all, being motivated by an understood morality and not by built-in sensations is what distinguishes people from animals.

Crowstone wrote:
Shitting will be quite useful for this job

You have no idea what the logic behind that is.
The author also wants to know but feels he might regret asking.
Dfaran wrote:
>You've got to go emit waste byproducts. Wouldn't want to have to do that when you're on the job, would you?

While your enzymes copied from many forms of life, combined with an internal recirculation system, can break down food almost completely to a usable form, there are definitely diminishing returns, so sometimes you have to shit.


Certain of the genome maintainers take pride in fitting thousand of species' diets into one. Being able to eat most plastics is quite reasonable; ditto cellulose if you're more desperate; but elemental aluminium is getting a bit silly. The fact that some bacteria can thrive off it is not a good excuse, and some more pragmatic genome variants (such as your own) omit dozens of the "standard" digestive enzymes.

Since the completists have not achieved their goal for the species, and in particular not for you, you head for the toilets by turning left out of your room. The lifts are that way anyway.

Faden wrote:
Fly there!

You walk out onto the take-off ledge; there is a drop on three sides.



You prepare as you have many times before: check energy level (plenty if you include the food you ate earlier), double-unfold your wings, test main actuators, test finger actuators, check spinal flexion, put respiration up to high, divert power away from non-essential systems, begin increasing wing and back-leg muscles' energy storage, warm up pectorals to sprint temperature (49 deg C), etc. While this cannot be automated, at least you can put the whole procedure into a lower module of your mind, with its pattern-anticipation learning turned off. Having to do all the checks consciously would just be too error-prone due to all the repetition.

Pre-flight is OK so you step back, run, and take off. You drop several metres during the initial sprint of flapping; from flat ground you couldn't have taken off at all with this load. You still don't have the spare power to think hard or to keep digesting the food you ate. Not great, but this isn't a long flight. It'll only take a couple of hours.

You look down at the same scenery that is always there (well, almost the same).

The author thinks this is a bit too wordy and not enough picturey, and a pre-planned journey is not the most open of scenarios unless something goes wrong.

The adventure has managed to hit the 1-week mark with daily updates so far. What is the mortality rate for these things anyway?
